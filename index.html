Ocean Crossing
Atlantic Crossing (Westbound)
Standard Passage  ·  Last reviewed: April 2026
The classic westbound transatlantic passage from Europe to the Caribbean via the Canary Islands, following the northeast trade winds. This is the most common superyacht repositioning passage, undertaken annually by hundreds of vessels at the end of the Mediterranean summer season.
🌊
Clear
Passage Intel
Atlantic hurricane season runs 1 June to 30 November. Outside this window, the trade wind crossing is a standard low-risk passage. Gibraltar fuel: duty-free bunkers available. Las Palmas: full staging services operational. No current advisories affecting the westbound transatlantic route.

Routing
▼

Recommended Route: Gibraltar (duty-free fuel) → Madeira or direct to Canary Islands (Las Palmas) → optional Cape Verde (Mindelo) → Caribbean landfall (Barbados, St Lucia, Antigua, or St Maarten). The route arcs south-southwest from the Canaries to approximately 18-20°N before turning west, following the trade wind belt. Do not attempt a direct great circle route — it passes through the Azores High where calms can strand a vessel for days.

Ship traffic is light mid-ocean but increases significantly approaching the Caribbean and near the Canaries.

🌊
Weather and Sea State
▼

Prevailing Conditions: Northeast trade winds 15-25 knots. Following seas with long-period Atlantic swell (2-3m, occasionally 4m). Air temperature 22-28°C. Sea temperature 22-26°C. Generally sunny with occasional squall activity.

Seasonal Variation: November: trades establishing but can be unstable, some residual low-pressure systems may reach the Canaries latitude. December-January: strongest and most consistent trades (the "Christmas trades"). February-March: trades begin to weaken as the Azores High shifts.

Storm Risk: Atlantic hurricane season officially ends 30 November but late-season storms have occurred in December. Monitor NOAA NHC advisories before departure. Once in the trade wind belt south of approximately 25°N, the risk of encountering a tropical system is very low.

Current: The Canary Current flows south along the African coast, assisting the initial southward leg. The North Equatorial Current flows west across the Atlantic between 10-20°N, providing a favourable set of 0.5-1.0 knots westward.

⛽
Fuel Planning
▼

Bunkering Points: Gibraltar (duty-free — cheapest on the route), Las Palmas (Canaries), Mindelo (Cape Verde — more expensive), Caribbean landfall port.

Consumption Guidance: Most superyachts will motor-sail or motor the crossing. At 10-12 knots economical speed, a 60m motor yacht burning 300-500 litres/hour will consume approximately 100,000-150,000 litres for the crossing. Ensure fuel capacity provides a minimum 20 per cent reserve above calculated consumption. Weather routing can significantly reduce fuel burn.

Emergency Fuel: There is no fuel available between the Canaries/Cape Verde and the Caribbean — approximately 2,100-2,700nm of open ocean. If fuel becomes critical, Bermuda is a potential diversion but adds significant distance northward and into different weather patterns.

📡
Communications
▼

GMDSS Areas: A1 (coastal) departing the Canaries, transitioning to A3 (Inmarsat satellite) for the ocean crossing. Continuous A3 coverage throughout. Inmarsat, Starlink Maritime, VSAT, and Iridium all provide continuous coverage across the Atlantic.

Cell Coverage: Ends within 20-30nm of the Canaries. No cell coverage for 14-21 days until approaching the Caribbean (20-30nm from landfall).

Weather Services: NOAA NHC (tropical weather), UK Met Office, Météo France. Professional weather routing services (Commanders Weather, Weather Routing Inc, PredictWind) provide daily route recommendations. GRIB files via satellite for onboard analysis.

Guest Expectations: Brief guests that cell phone service will be unavailable for 2-3 weeks. Satellite broadband (Starlink or VSAT) provides internet but bandwidth is limited compared to shore. Streaming video may be impractical. Set expectations before departure.

Insurance and Security
▼

Insurance Zones: No JWC listed areas crossed. No INL boundary crossings. The entire route is within standard navigational limits. Standard yacht insurance covers this passage without endorsement.

Security: Negligible threat. The mid-Atlantic has no piracy, no military activity, no restricted zones on the standard trade wind route. This is one of the safest ocean passages in the world.

Named Storm: Confirm named-storm cover if departing before 30 November (official end of Atlantic hurricane season).

🌿
Environmental
▼

ECA: Not within any ECA. Global 0.5 per cent sulphur cap applies throughout.

Discharge: MARPOL open ocean regulations apply. No discharge within 12nm of any land. Food waste may be discharged more than 12nm from land if ground to particles under 25mm.

Wildlife: Cetacean encounters (whales, dolphins) are common, particularly in the eastern Atlantic and approaching the Caribbean. Maintain lookout and reduce speed if whales sighted.

Chokepoint Transit
Suez Canal Transit
Restrictions Apply  ·  Last reviewed: April 2026
The Suez Canal connects the Mediterranean (Port Said) to the Red Sea (Port of Suez), eliminating the 6,000nm Cape of Good Hope route. Since late 2023, Houthi attacks in the southern Red Sea have complicated the security picture — the canal itself remains safe, but the onward Red Sea passage is the concern.
⚠️
Caution
Suez / Red Sea Intel
Canal transit: operational, no disruption. Red Sea onward: elevated threat. Houthi truce announced but does not cover Israeli-linked vessels. JWLA-033 (3 March 2026) expanded listed areas. Transit volumes approximately 12,758 in 2025 (down from 26,434 in 2023). War risk premium: 0.5-1.5 per cent of hull value. Alternative: Cape of Good Hope route adds 4,800-6,000nm and 20-30 days but avoids all JWC zones.

Transit Procedures
▼

Booking: Transit must be booked through a Suez Canal agent. The agent submits vessel details to the SCA at least 48 hours before the intended transit. Longer advance notice is advisable to secure a preferred convoy slot.

Documentation: Ship registration certificate, insurance certificate (P&I club letter of undertaking for canal transit), crew list, passenger list, last port clearance, tonnage certificate. The SCA calculates fees based on Suez Canal Net Tonnage (SCNT).

Pilotage: Mandatory. An SCA pilot boards at the entry point and remains throughout. The yacht must accommodate the pilot (cabin, meals). Pilot changes may occur en route.

Convoy: Yachts typically transit as part of a convoy. Timing is set by the SCA — the yacht must be at the staging anchorage at the appointed time. Delays are not tolerated.

Overnight: Some transits include an overnight stop at the Great Bitter Lake (midway). The yacht anchors and resumes with the next day's convoy.

Agent: Mandatory. Appoint at least 2 weeks before the intended transit. Established agents: Inchcape Shipping Services, Felix Shipping. Expect USD 1,500-5,000.

🧭
Navigation
▼

Dimensions: Length 193km (104nm). Width 205m minimum. Maximum vessel draft 20.1m (66ft) — not a constraint for any superyacht. Maximum air draft 68m (Suez Canal Bridge) — not a constraint for any superyacht. Maximum beam 77.5m.

Speed: Maximum set by the SCA, typically 7-8 knots for yachts. Follow pilot instructions precisely. Wake generation is a serious concern — damage to canal banks can result in fines.

Hazards: Sand ingestion in engines and watermakers is a significant issue — the canal water is extremely sandy. Close raw water intakes for non-essential systems during transit. Strong crosswinds at the El Ballah bypass. Heavy commercial ship traffic — maintain station as directed by the pilot. Do not attempt to overtake.

Entry Points: Port Said (Mediterranean end) — anchorage in the outer harbour. Port of Suez / Port Tewfik (Red Sea end) — anchorage off Suez.

⚠️
Security
▼

Canal Security: The Suez Canal itself is secure. Egyptian military and SCA security patrol the waterway. ISPS procedures apply. No incidents affecting vessels within the canal.

Red Sea Onward: The critical concern is the onward passage through the Red Sea after exiting southbound. The southern Red Sea and Gulf of Aden have been subject to Houthi missile, drone, and naval drone attacks since November 2023. As of April 2026, a Houthi-announced truce is in effect but does not cover Israeli-linked vessels, and hostilities could resume.

Decision Point: For southbound transits (Med to Indian Ocean): the canal is safe, but the operator must decide whether to continue into the Red Sea or divert via the Cape. This decision has major insurance, routing, and scheduling implications.

Reporting: UKMTO voluntary reporting for vessels transiting the Red Sea. Register and provide regular position reports.

🛡️
Insurance
▼

Canal Transit: Egyptian territorial waters — not JWC listed. The SCA requires a P&I club letter of undertaking specifically for canal transit (agent arranges).

Red Sea Exit: Exiting the canal southbound enters the JWC listed area (JWLA-033, 3 March 2026). Notify war risk underwriter before entering. Additional premium required — rates for Red Sea passage can add 0.5-1.5 per cent of hull value per transit.

H&M Limits: The Red Sea may be outside standard IWL. Confirm with your H&M underwriter. Do not enter listed area waters without written confirmation of cover.

Critical: Some vessels have been unable to obtain war risk cover for the southern Red Sea at any price during peak threat periods. Confirm insurance before committing to the transit.

Alternatives
▼

Cape of Good Hope Route: The alternative to the Suez Canal and Red Sea is approximately 6,000nm longer from the Med to the Indian Ocean, adding 20-30 days of passage time and significant fuel cost. However, it avoids the JWC listed area, the war risk premium, and the Houthi threat. Since late 2023, an increasing number of superyachts have chosen the Cape route.

Cape Route Challenges: The Agulhas Current off South Africa, heavy weather in the Southern Ocean, and limited bunkering options on the west coast of Africa.

Timing: If the Red Sea security situation improves and the Houthi threat is resolved, the Suez transit returns to being the standard, straightforward passage it has been for decades. Monitor the situation and make the decision as late as practically possible.

🌿
Environmental
▼

Discharge: No discharge in the canal. All waste retained on board. The SCA has introduced mandatory waste collection for transiting vessels (as of May 2025).

Note: The canal is an artificial waterway through the desert — no marine ecosystem concerns within the canal. Environmental considerations resume upon entering the Red Sea (coral reef protection, marine park regulations).

Canal Transit
Panama Canal Transit
Restrictions Apply  ·  Last reviewed: April 2026
The Panama Canal connects the Atlantic (Caribbean) and Pacific oceans via a system of locks, lifting vessels 26m above sea level across Gatun Lake. It is the primary passage for superyachts repositioning between the Caribbean and the Pacific (Galapagos, French Polynesia, Alaska). Yachts have the lowest scheduling priority — plan well ahead.

Transit Procedures
▼

Lock System: Three sets of twin locks — Gatun (Atlantic side), Pedro Miguel and Miraflores (Pacific side). The canal lifts vessels 26m (85ft) to Gatun Lake, then lowers them back to sea level. Neopanamax locks (Agua Clara and Cocoli) opened in 2016 but are for larger commercial vessels.

Handline Transit: Vessels under 125ft (38.1m) transit as "handlines" — secured to lock walls by lines handled by crew and hired line handlers on deck. Vessels under 65ft use a transit advisor; vessels 65ft and over require a pilot. Superyachts over 125ft transit under full pilotage like commercial vessels.

Equipment Required: Four mooring lines minimum 125ft (38m) long and 7/8 inch diameter. Eight heavy-duty fenders (standard inflatable fenders are insufficient — use tyre fenders or heavy-duty). Four competent line handlers (excluding captain). Lines, fenders, and line handlers can be hired locally through your agent.

Booking: Register on the ACP's ASEM portal. Virtual admeasurement now accepted for most vessels under 125ft (physical inspection may still occur). Transit must be booked at least 96 hours in advance. Yachts have the lowest scheduling priority — waits of 3-21 days are possible for unbooked transits. Agent strongly recommended to navigate the system.

Panamanian Flag: All vessels must display the Panamanian flag while in canal waters (dawn to 1800). Non-compliance may delay scheduling.

Agent: Strongly recommended. Handles ASEM registration, admeasurement, booking, line handler coordination, pilotage, customs, and all logistics. Sea Masters, Associated Yacht Services, Waypoint Yacht Services, Oliver Yacht Agents are established operators. Expect USD 450-2,000 for agent fees depending on vessel size.

Navigation
▼

Dimensions (Original Locks): Maximum beam 32.3m (106ft). Maximum draft 12.04m (39.5ft) in tropical fresh water. Maximum LOA 294m. These are the locks most superyachts use.

Dimensions (Neopanamax Locks): Maximum beam 49m (161ft). Maximum draft 15.2m (50ft). Maximum LOA 366m. Used by larger commercial vessels.

Gatun Lake: After ascending through the Gatun Locks, vessels cross Gatun Lake — a 38km (20.5nm) freshwater lake 26m above sea level. Navigational buoys mark the channel. The lake transit is the longest portion of the journey.

Hazards: Lock chambers create powerful water turbulence — heavy fender protection essential. The vessel may be rafted alongside other yachts in the locks. Maintain engine power and steering throughout. Rain squalls can reduce visibility on Gatun Lake. Commercial ship traffic — maintain safe distance. Fresh water in Gatun Lake means the vessel sits lower than in salt water.

Entry Points: Atlantic side: Cristobal / Colon (anchor at designated areas in Bahia Limon). Pacific side: Balboa / Panama City (Flamenco Marina or La Playita anchorage).

🛡️
Insurance and Security
▼

Insurance: No JWC listed areas. Within standard INL. Some H&M policies specifically exclude or require notification for canal lock transits due to the risk of hull contact — confirm with your broker. P&I club letter of undertaking may be required by the ACP.

Security: Panama Canal Zone is secure. The ACP and Panamanian police maintain order. Colon (Atlantic side) has a higher crime rate than Panama City — standard precautions, secure tenders. Panama City (Pacific side) is generally safe and has excellent amenities.

Corinth Canal Transit
Check Status Before Routing  ·  Last reviewed: April 2026
The Corinth Canal is a narrow, 6.4km cut through the Isthmus of Corinth, connecting the Gulf of Corinth (Ionian Sea) with the Saronic Gulf (Aegean Sea). It saves approximately 175nm versus rounding the Peloponnese. However, repeated landslide closures and a narrow beam limit mean this canal requires careful planning — many superyachts are simply too wide to transit. As of April 2026, the canal is closed for restoration works with reopening expected around June-July 2026.

Transit Procedures
▼

Booking: Contact the canal operator (AEDIK S.A.) or arrange through your Greek agent. Bookings can often be made same-day if the canal is operational. Vessels wait at designated anchorages at either end (Posidonia on the Saronic/Aegean side, Isthmia).

Pilotage: Not required for yachts — the canal is a straight, narrow cut. The vessel transits under its own power at slow speed. One-way traffic only — vessels wait for the direction to be cleared.

Dimensions: Canal width 24.6m at sea level, 21.0m at the bottom. Maximum vessel beam 18.3m (60ft). Maximum draft 7.2m. Air draft 54m (road bridge), 58.8m (rail bridge). The walls are 79m high limestone cliffs — dramatically narrow and steep. Maximum speed 6 knots. One-way traffic only — vessels wait for the direction to be cleared. VHF Ch 11 for canal control.

Current Status (April 2026): The canal is closed for restoration works since October 2025. Reopening expected around June-July 2026, pending official confirmation. The canal operator is upgrading systems for continuous 24/7/365 operations. Check status with AEDIK S.A. (corinth_canal@aedik.gr) or your agent before routing.

Cost: Transit fees are modest — approximately EUR 15-30 per metre of vessel length. A 40m yacht would pay approximately EUR 600-1,200. Agent fee additional.

⚠️
Closures and Hazards
▼

Landslide History: The canal's steep limestone walls are prone to rockfalls and landslides. Major closures occurred in 2021-2023 for stabilisation works. The canal has reopened but further closures remain possible at short notice. The geological instability is a permanent feature of the canal — it was carved through unstable rock in the 1880s.

Reduced Dimensions: After landslide events, the canal depth and width may be temporarily reduced due to debris. Confirm current dimensions with the operator before transit if your vessel has limited clearance.

Current: Tidal currents can flow through the canal and may affect handling, particularly for sailing yachts under engine. Current direction and strength vary — ask the operator for current conditions.

Alternative: If the canal is closed or your vessel is too wide, the route around the southern Peloponnese adds approximately 130nm. The passage rounds Cape Matapan (Tainaron) — the southernmost point of mainland Greece — which can have rough conditions in the Meltemi wind season (July-August).

🛡️
Insurance and Security
▼

Insurance: No JWC listing. Within standard INL. The narrow canal presents a hull contact risk — confirm your H&M policy covers canal transits. The limestone walls are unforgiving. Fender protection is essential.

Security: No security concerns. The canal is in a populated area of Greece with no crime risk.

Cape Horn (55°59'S) is the southernmost point of South America. The Drake Passage between Cape Horn and the Antarctic Peninsula is approximately 500nm of the most demanding open water on Earth — persistent westerly gales, massive seas, strong currents, and ice. This passage is relevant for Patagonia/Antarctica itineraries and for the rare Pacific-Atlantic repositioning avoiding Panama.

Patagonia & Antarctica
🌊
Weather and Sea State
▼

Prevailing Conditions: Persistent westerly winds 25-45 knots (the "Roaring Forties," "Furious Fifties," and "Screaming Sixties"). Seas 4-8m routinely, 10-12m in storm conditions. Long-period Southern Ocean swell with no land to break it for thousands of miles. Water temperature 2-6°C. Air temperature 0-8°C in summer.

Drake Passage: The narrowing between South America and Antarctica funnels the Antarctic Circumpolar Current and associated weather systems into a 500nm-wide gauntlet. Conditions can change from calm to storm in hours. The "Drake Lake" (rare calm crossings) and the "Drake Shake" (the norm) are both well-documented.

Ice: Icebergs and growlers possible throughout the Drake Passage, increasing closer to Antarctica. Radar watch essential 24/7. Visual lookout supplemented by radar and, if available, ice detection software. Night transits through ice fields are extremely dangerous.

Weather Routing: Professional weather routing is not optional — it is essential. The routing service will identify weather windows for the crossing and advise on optimal departure timing. Budget for 2-5 days of waiting at Ushuaia for a window.

🛡️
Insurance and Vessel Requirements
▼

INL Exclusion: South of 50°S is permanently excluded under International Navigating Limits. A specific endorsement from your H&M underwriter is required. Substantial additional premium — potentially 2-5 per cent of hull value for the Antarctic portion.

Polar Code: Pleasure yachts of 300GT and above must hold a Polar Code Ship Certificate (from January 2026). Existing vessels must comply by January 2027. The certificate requires an operational risk assessment, a Polar Water Operational Manual, and flag state survey.

Vessel Requirements: Ice-strengthened or ice-rated hull. Redundant propulsion and power systems. Cold water survival equipment for all persons on board (immersion suits, not just life jackets). Ship's doctor mandatory for Antarctic voyages. EPIRB, SART, AIS, comprehensive GMDSS radio installation.

IAATO: For voyages to Antarctica, IAATO (International Association of Antarctica Tour Operators) compliance is strongly recommended. IAATO sets standards for environmental protection, safety, and operational best practice.

⛽
Fuel and Logistics
▼

Routing
▼

Agulhas Current: The warm Agulhas Current flows southwest along the continental shelf. When opposing strong westerly swells and wind, it creates extreme sea conditions — abnormally steep, short-period waves and documented rogue waves. This is one of the most dangerous sea areas in the world in adverse conditions. Weather routing is essential to time the rounding for favourable conditions.

Bunkering en route: West Africa has limited reliable bunkering options for yachts. Dakar (Senegal), Luanda (Angola), and Walvis Bay (Namibia) are possible but not yacht-oriented. Cape Town is the primary staging port. East side: Durban and Richards Bay.

🌊
Weather and Sea State
▼

Cape region: The Cape of Good Hope sits at approximately 34°S where Southern Ocean weather systems interact with the African continent. Strong westerly and southwesterly gales are common, particularly in the austral winter (June-August). Summer (December-February) offers the most settled conditions but storms can occur year-round.

Rogue waves: The Agulhas Current area off the Wild Coast (between Durban and East London) is one of the world's documented rogue wave zones. Waves exceeding 20m have been recorded. This is not theoretical — vessels have been damaged and lost in this area.

Season: The passage can be made year-round but the austral summer (November-March) offers the most favourable conditions. Time the rounding itself around weather windows — expect to wait 2-5 days in Cape Town for optimal conditions.

South Africa: no maritime security concerns. Piracy consideration: If routing via the west coast of Africa, maintain AIS on, radar watch, and standard anti-piracy precautions through the Gulf of Guinea. The risk to superyachts is low but not zero.

Routing
▼

Route Options: Three main routes exist. (1) Azores Route (most popular): Caribbean → arc northeast, staying south of 30°N until 40°W, then north to Azores (Horta, Faial). From Azores, hop to mainland Portugal or Gibraltar. (2) Bermuda-Azores Route: Caribbean → Bermuda (850nm) → Azores (1,800nm) → Europe (1,200nm). Breaks the journey and provides weather routing flexibility. (3) Direct Route: Experienced crews only with ample fuel reserves, heading directly for the Azores or even mainland Europe.

The Azores High: The dominant weather feature. The high-pressure system sits mid-Atlantic and must be navigated around — getting trapped in it means days of calm. Stay south of the high, then arc north to pick up westerlies for the final approach to the Azores.

Departure Points: St Maarten and Tortola (BVI) are the most popular — well-positioned, good provisioning, chandlery, and repairs. Antigua also popular (ARC Europe rally departs from here).

Hazards: Weather systems spinning off the US East Coast bring lows and frontal systems that extend south. At some point you will be overtaken by at least one front. The passage is "largely upwind" with variable conditions — a very different character from the downhill westbound crossing. Prepare for heavy weather.

🌊
Weather and Sea State
▼

Prevailing Conditions: Variable — this is the key difference from the westbound crossing. Confused seas possible where weather systems interact.

Seasonal Window: May to mid-June. April departures push into active late-season storm patterns. After mid-June, hurricane season risk increases. The window is narrow — perhaps 4-6 weeks. May departures are generally preferred.

Gulf Stream: If routing via the US East Coast or Bermuda, the Gulf Stream provides a favourable push of 2-4 knots but also creates rough seas when wind opposes current.

Weather Routing: Professional weather routing is essential — not just helpful but genuinely necessary. The routing service identifies weather windows, advises on departure timing, and provides daily route adjustments. Commanders Weather, Chris Parker's Marine Weather Center, PredictWind are all experienced with this crossing.

Crew Change: Bermuda (L.F. Wade International Airport — direct to US East Coast and London). Azores (Horta — flights to Lisbon, 2h30). Gibraltar or Lisbon on arrival in Europe.

Provisioning: Provision fully in the Caribbean before departure. Bermuda has good provisioning if stopping. Azores: Horta has adequate supplies but limited specialist provisioning. Routing and Monsoon
▼

Northeast Monsoon (November-March): Winds from the northeast, 15-25 knots. Favourable for westbound passages (Indonesia/Maldives toward Africa/Red Sea). Relatively settled conditions. The traditional "cruising season" for the western Indian Ocean.

Southwest Monsoon (May-September): Winds from the southwest, 20-35 knots. Much stronger and more boisterous than the NE monsoon. Favourable for eastbound passages (Africa toward Maldives/Indonesia). The Somali coast experiences the strongest winds. Significant seas — 3-5m common.

Transition Periods (April, October): Variable winds, occasional calms, thunderstorms. Brief windows for cross-monsoon passages. These are the least predictable times.

Cyclone Season: The Indian Ocean has two cyclone basins. The western basin (affecting Seychelles, Madagascar, Mauritius) is active November-April. The eastern basin (affecting Indonesia, northern Australia) is active November-April. The Maldives sits between the basins — cyclone risk is low but not zero.

Standard Route (westbound): Indonesia → Maldives → Seychelles → Gulf of Aden → Red Sea. Typically completed December-March during the NE monsoon.

Somali Piracy: Piracy has diminished significantly since 2012 but the JWC maintains its listed area. The risk to yachts transiting at speed on a direct route is low but not zero. Maintain AIS, radar watch, and standard anti-piracy precautions. Consider an armed escort for Gulf of Aden transit if routing through.

Open Indian Ocean: Maldives, Seychelles, and Indonesian waters are not JWC listed. Standard insurance cover applies for passages between these destinations.

Bunkering: Fuel available in the Maldives (Malé), Seychelles (Victoria), and Indonesian ports. Red Sea: Port Ghalib, Aden (with security caveats). Plan fuel carefully for the longer open-ocean legs.

Routing
▼

Route: From the Galapagos, the route heads southwest across the equator into the southeast trade wind belt. The crossing passes through the ITCZ (Inter-Tropical Convergence Zone) — a band of calms, squalls, and thunderstorms — typically within the first 2-3 days. Once through the ITCZ and into the southeast trades (typically around 5-8°S), conditions settle into steady following winds and seas for the remainder.

ITCZ: The doldrums. Expect 100-300nm of light winds, calms, squalls, and heavy rain. Motor through as directly as practical. The ITCZ position varies seasonally — check satellite imagery and weather routing for current position before departure.

Currents: The South Equatorial Current flows west at approximately 0.5-1.0 knots, providing a favourable set for the entire crossing once through the ITCZ. The Humboldt Current (cold, from Antarctica) influences the eastern portion near the Galapagos.

Landfall: Hiva Oa (Atuona) or Nuku Hiva (Taiohae) in the Marquesas Islands. Both have customs clearance facilities. Hiva Oa is the traditional first landfall — closer and with Gauguin's grave as a cultural landmark.

⛽
Fuel and Self-Sufficiency
▼

Critical: There is nothing between the Galapagos and the Marquesas — 3,000nm of open ocean. No islands, no shipping lanes, no rescue infrastructure. The vessel must carry fuel, water, provisions, and spares for the entire crossing plus a minimum 25 per cent reserve.

Fuel: Last bunkering at Baltra (Galapagos — government-controlled, permit required). No fuel available in the Marquesas except limited supply at Taiohae (Nuku Hiva) and Atuona (Hiva Oa). A motor yacht at 10-12kt burning 300-500l/h will consume 90,000-180,000 litres. Confirm fuel capacity provides adequate range plus reserve. Watermakers essential and must be fully operational.

Provisioning: Provision in the Galapagos is very limited — ideally provision fully before arriving in the Galapagos (mainland Ecuador or Panama). Carry frozen and dry goods for 3 weeks minimum. Fishing supplements provisions (tuna, mahi-mahi abundant).

Spares: Carry comprehensive spares. There is no rescue, no towing service, and no marine engineer between the Galapagos and French Polynesia. A critical mechanical failure mid-passage is a serious emergency.

📡
Communications
▼

GMDSS: Sea Area A3 (Inmarsat) for the entire crossing. All satellite systems operational. Iridium provides continuous global coverage (essential backup).

Cell Coverage: None for the entire crossing — approximately 2-3 weeks without cell service. Brief guests thoroughly before departure.

Remoteness: This is one of the most remote stretches of ocean on Earth. SAR response from either end would take days. EPIRB registration, satellite phone, and AIS are essential. Notify a shore contact of your passage plan and expected arrival window.

Departure Point: Most yachts depart from the Brest peninsula or Channel Islands to minimise the open-water crossing. Wait at Brest, Camaret, or L'Aber Wrac'h for a weather window of at least 48-72 hours of settled conditions.

Portuguese Trades: Once past Cape Finisterre (NW Spain), the Portuguese trade winds set in during summer — strong northerly/northwesterly winds along the Portuguese coast. This can make the southbound leg fast but uncomfortable. Expect 20-30kt northerlies, particularly in the afternoon.

🌊
Weather and Sea State
▼

Reputation: Biscay's reputation for rough conditions is well-earned. Atlantic depressions track across the bay, generating steep, confused seas. The continental shelf edge creates particularly uncomfortable conditions when swell meets shallow water. Seas of 4-6m are common in unsettled weather; 8-10m in storms.

Summer (Jun-Aug): The most settled period. High pressure often extends from the Azores, providing light to moderate winds and calm seas. However, summer gales do occur — always check the forecast. Sea temperature 15-20°C.

Shoulder Seasons (May, Sep-Oct): Increasingly variable. Atlantic depressions start tracking further south. Weather windows become shorter.

Winter (Nov-Apr): Frequent gales and storms. Not recommended for voluntary passage. Yachts repositioning in this season should plan for extended waits and heavy weather.

Weather Routing: Check the 5-day forecast before committing to a crossing. UK Met Office shipping forecast for Biscay, FitzRoy, and Sole. Météo France marine forecasts. A 48-hour window of settled conditions is the minimum for a comfortable crossing.

🛡️
Environmental and Shipping
▼

ECA: The Bay of Biscay is outside the North Sea ECA — the ECA boundary ends at approximately 48°30'N (Ushant/Channel approaches). Biscay is subject to the global 0.5 per cent sulphur cap only. Note however that departing from the English Channel or approaching from the north, you will transit through the North Sea ECA (0.10 per cent sulphur) before entering Biscay.

Shipping: Heavy commercial traffic, particularly around the TSS off Ushant (one of the busiest shipping lanes in the world) and approaching the Strait of Gibraltar. Maintain radar and AIS watch. The Ushant TSS has mandatory reporting for vessels over 300GT.

Fishing: Dense fishing fleet activity, particularly on the French continental shelf. Fishing vessels may not respond to VHF calls and may have unexpected manoeuvres. Maintain lookout at night.

Routing
▼

Via Faroe Islands (recommended): The Faroe Islands provide a natural staging point mid-way between Scandinavia/Scotland and Iceland. Bergen/Shetland → Faroe Islands (~350nm) → Iceland (~380-420nm). This breaks the passage into manageable legs with a bail-out option in Tórshavn.

Direct routes: Bergen → Reykjavik (~850nm), Scotland → Reykjavik (~700nm). These require 3-5 days of sustained open-water passage with no intermediate shelter.

Bail-out options: Limited. Once committed to the open Norwegian Sea, the Faroe Islands are the only intermediate option. If conditions deteriorate, turning back may be the only safe choice. Do not commit to the passage without a 72-hour weather window.

Faroe Islands: Tórshavn provides fuel, provisions, and shelter. Customs clearance required (Faroe Islands are a self-governing territory of Denmark, outside the EU). Good harbour with dramatic cliff scenery.

🌊
Weather and Sea State
▼

Conditions: The Norwegian Sea is exposed to the full force of North Atlantic weather systems. Even in summer, depressions cross the area regularly. Winds 15-30kt common; gale force in unsettled weather. Seas 3-5m routinely, higher in storm conditions. Sea temperature 6-12°C. Air temperature 5-15°C in summer.

Fog: Common in summer when warm Atlantic air meets cold Arctic water. Visibility can drop to near zero for extended periods. Radar essential.

Daylight: The great advantage of summer passage — near-continuous daylight (20-24 hours) in June and July. This aids navigation, watchkeeping, and morale.

Cold Water: Water temperature 6-12°C. Cold water survival equipment mandatory for all crew. Immersion without survival suits is life-threatening within minutes. Brief crew on cold water safety.

Weather Routing: Professional routing or at minimum expert interpretation of ECMWF/Met Office charts. The Norwegian Meteorological Institute (yr.no) provides excellent open-access forecasting for this area.

⛽
Fuel and Logistics
▼

Bunkering: Bergen (Norway), Tórshavn (Faroe Islands), Reykjavik (Iceland). All have reliable diesel supply. Iceland fuel is expensive (ISK 300-350/litre, approximately EUR 1.80-2.10).

Crew Change: Bergen (BGO — good European connections). Tórshavn (FAE — flights to Copenhagen). Reykjavik (KEF — direct to London 3h, New York 5h30).

Communications: GMDSS Sea Area A2 (MF) near coasts, A3 (Inmarsat) offshore. Cell coverage at ports only. NAVTEX provides weather warnings. Norwegian and Icelandic coast guards monitor VHF Ch 16.

ECA: The North Sea ECA applies to Norwegian coastal waters. Switch to 0.10 per cent sulphur fuel before entering Norwegian waters if arriving from outside the ECA.

Canal Transit
Panama Canal
Operational

The Panama Canal connects the Atlantic and Pacific via a system of locks. Superyachts transit regularly between the Caribbean and the Pacific (Galapagos, French Polynesia, Alaska). Booking through an agent is required. Transit takes 8-10 hours.

Key data: Three sets of locks (Gatun, Pedro Miguel, Miraflores) plus the Neopanamax locks. Maximum beam 32.3m (old locks) or 49m (new locks). Maximum draft 12.04m (tropical fresh water). Maximum LOA 294m (old) or 366m (new). Most superyachts use the original locks.

Recent context: Drought conditions in 2023-2024 forced the Panama Canal Authority to restrict daily transits and reduce permitted drafts. Conditions have since improved but the canal remains sensitive to rainfall levels in Gatun Lake. Booking slots for yachts may be limited during peak commercial demand.

Suez Canal
Open — Red Sea Caution

The Suez Canal itself is fully operational and secure. The concern is the onward passage: the southern Red Sea and Gulf of Aden remain under elevated threat from Houthi forces. A truce has been announced but does not cover Israeli-linked vessels and could collapse.

Transit volumes: Dropped from 26,434 transits in 2023 to approximately 12,758 in 2025 due to Red Sea diversions. Many superyachts are avoiding the Red Sea entirely and repositioning via the Cape of Good Hope.

Insurance: JWC listed area (JWLA-033, 3 March 2026) begins at the Red Sea exit. War risk premium 0.5-1.5 per cent of hull value per transit. Confirm cover before committing.

Rounding
Cape Horn
Seasonal — Weather Dependent

Cape Horn (55°59'S) is the southernmost point of South America and the passage between the Atlantic and Pacific oceans. It is relevant for superyachts on Chilean Patagonia itineraries and for the rare Pacific-Atlantic repositioning that avoids Panama.

Conditions: Among the most dangerous waters on Earth. The Drake Passage south of Cape Horn experiences persistent westerly gales, massive seas (6-10m common), strong currents, and poor visibility. Water temperature 4-8°C. Icebergs possible.

Season: December to March only (Southern Hemisphere summer). Even in summer, weather windows are unpredictable. Professional weather routing essential. The vessel must be ice-strengthened or ice-rated for any passage south of 50°S.

Insurance: South of 50°S is permanently excluded under INL. Specific endorsement required from H&M underwriter.

Rounding
Cape of Good Hope
Open — Standard Passage

The Cape of Good Hope route around southern Africa has become increasingly relevant since 2023 as an alternative to the Suez Canal / Red Sea route for Med-to-Indian Ocean repositioning. It adds approximately 4,800-6,000nm and 20-30 days compared to the Suez route.

Conditions: The Agulhas Current off the southeast coast of South Africa creates some of the most dangerous sea conditions in the world when it opposes strong westerly swells. Rogue waves are a documented phenomenon in this area. Cape Town provides an excellent staging port with full superyacht services.

Bunkering: Cape Town is the primary fuel stop. West African coast bunkering options are limited (Luanda, Walvis Bay). East coast: Durban, Richards Bay.

Insurance: Within standard INL. No JWC listing. No war risk concerns. Standard cover applies. This is the key insurance advantage over the Suez/Red Sea route.

Strait Transit
Strait of Malacca
Open

The Strait of Malacca (550nm) between the Malay Peninsula and Sumatra connects the Indian Ocean to the South China Sea and Pacific. Relevant for superyachts repositioning between the Indian Ocean (Maldives, Seychelles) and Southeast Asia or Japan.

Traffic: One of the busiest shipping lanes in the world (approximately 100,000 vessel transits per year). Commercial ship traffic is extremely dense. Maintain full radar and AIS watch. Traffic separation schemes apply.

Security: Piracy incidents have decreased significantly since the mid-2000s. The strait is actively patrolled by Malaysian, Singaporean, and Indonesian navies. The risk to superyachts is low but maintain standard anti-piracy precautions (AIS on, lights on, radar watch, doors locked at night).

Singapore: The southern end of the strait exits into the Singapore Strait — one of the world's great superyacht hubs. Full provisioning, repair, crew change, and bunkering in Singapore.
